## Case Study: Temporal Echoes in Exoplanetary Systems

**Event Overview**
In 2092 the transit light curve of the super‑Earth K2‑18b displayed a repeat absorption feature at 1.4 µm that matched a spectral line predicted for the year 2120 from climate models of CO₂ accumulation. The repeat appeared 28 years after the initial detection, indicating a backward‑in‑time signal.

The suspension duration τ = ħ/(k_B ΔS) ≈ 2.4 × 10⁻³ s permitted the future atmospheric state to imprint on the present transit spectrum. The inversion was traced to a passing dark‑matter filament of density ρ_DM = 0.27 g cm⁻³.

**Impact on Celestial Bodies**
The echo altered the inferred atmospheric composition, causing climate models to project a runaway greenhouse episode beginning in 2135. Models that omitted the echo overestimated surface temperatures by 12 K.

**Implications for Planetary Evolution**
The event shows that temporal echoes can inject future climate states into present observations, affecting habitability assessments. It also demonstrates that dark‑matter filaments can anchor fractures that borrow entropy from future planetary conditions.

**Conclusion**
The K2‑18b echo exemplifies how exoplanetary observations may be compromised by temporal borrowing, requiring correction factors in atmospheric retrievals. Continued monitoring of similar transits will refine the entropy‑suspension formula and improve predictions of echo strength.
