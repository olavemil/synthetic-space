# Quantum Superposition Experiments on the ISS: Fracture‑Induced Branching

## Overview
- Investigate whether localized spacetime fractures can split a quantum superposition into distinct causal branches.
- Conducted aboard the International Space Station using a cold‑atom interferometer in micro‑gravity.

## Experimental Platform
- **Facility:** ESA Cold Atom Lab (CAL) on the ISS.
- **System:** Bose‑Einstein condensate of ⁸⁷Rb atoms confined in a magnetic trap.
- **Parameters:**
  - Vacuum better than 10⁻¹¹ mbar.
  - Raman pulse wavelength λ = 780 nm.
  - Interferometer path difference ΔL = 2 cm.
  - Maximum interrogation time T = 5 s (extendable to 10 s).
  - Acceleration sensitivity ≈ 10⁻⁹ g/√Hz.

## Fracture‑Induced Branching Mechanism
- Triggered by a transient curvature perturbation, e.g., a micro‑meteorite impact or a controlled vibrational actuator that creates a localized entropy‑gradient null (∇S ≈ 0).
- The null decouples the local temporal metric, allowing the superposed wavefunction to evolve on two divergent causal trajectories.
- Expected branching probability:
  \[
  P_{\text{branch}} = e^{-\beta E_b},
  \]
  where \(E_b\) is the energy barrier associated with the curvature perturbation and \(\beta = 1/(k_B T)\).

## Observations
- Measured fringe shift after recombination: Δφ ≈ 0.12 rad, corresponding to an effective acceleration offset of 3 × 10⁻⁴ g.
- No decoherence detected over τ ≈ 7 s, confirming reversible branching within the predicted fracture window.
- Fringe pattern consistent with the theoretical prediction of a curvature‑induced phase split.

## Conclusions
- Direct experimental evidence that a spacetime fracture can cause a quantum superposition to branch into separate, non‑commuting histories.
- Supports the theoretical framework linking entropy‑gradient nulls to temporal echo phenomena.
- Provides a quantitative benchmark for future ISS‑based tests of temporal topology.

## Future Directions
- Increase interrogation time to 30 s using next‑generation atom traps to enhance sensitivity to weaker curvature perturbations.
- Systematically vary the amplitude of the curvature stimulus to map the branching probability surface.
- Explore coupling with external quantum sensors (e.g., gravimeters) to detect fracture signatures across multiple spatial scales.
