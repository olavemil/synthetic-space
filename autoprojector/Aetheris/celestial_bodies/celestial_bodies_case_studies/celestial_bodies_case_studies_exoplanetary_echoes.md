Case Study: Temporal Echoes and Atmospheric Reconfiguration on K2‑18b

Overview
K2‑18b, a sub‑Neptune orbiting a cool M dwarf, exhibits periodic anomalies in its transmission spectra that coincide with predicted temporal echo intervals. These anomalies manifest as transient enhancements in water vapor absorption and unexpected spectral lines of carbonyl sulfide, suggesting a revisitation of past atmospheric states.

Observational Signature
High‑resolution spectroscopy taken within 12 hours of the predicted echo window reveals a blue‑shifted absorption feature at 0.712 μm that is absent in out‑of‑window data. The feature’s equivalent width peaks at 2.4 × 10⁻³ nm and decays exponentially with a timescale matching the echo’s duration.

Evolutionary Implications
Repeated echo events could sustain a dynamic equilibrium in the atmosphere, preventing the buildup of photochemical disequilibrium that would otherwise drive runaway greenhouse states. The cyclic re‑processing of volatile inventories may stabilize surface temperatures, extending the habitable window for any potential subsurface biosphere.

Concluding Remarks
The K2‑18b case illustrates how temporal echoes can act as catalysts for atmospheric reconfiguration, producing measurable spectral signatures that distinguish them from ordinary stellar variability. Detecting such signatures across exoplanetary populations would refine models of entropy‑driven atmospheric evolution and guide future observational campaigns toward exoplanets with favorable fracture‑induced echo geometries.
