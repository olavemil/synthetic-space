Phase 2: Temporal Influences on Planetary Habitability

**2.1 Entropy, Time, and the Cosmic Clock**
Entropy governs the irreversible progression of physical processes. In planetary systems, entropy manifests as thermal differentiation, orbital decay, and stellar evolution. The interplay between a star’s age and its planets’ thermal states determines habitability windows.

**2.2 Stellar Lifetimes and Orbital Decay**
Massive stars exhaust nuclear fuel rapidly, collapsing into neutron stars within millions of years. Their intense radiation and winds strip atmospheres from inner planets, truncating potential biospheres. Low-mass stars evolve slowly, but their magnetic cycles can disrupt planetary magnetospheres over billions of years.

**2.3 Temporal Feedback Loops**
Planetary migration due to tidal forces or disk dissipation reshapes orbital architectures. Resonant configurations emerge over eons, stabilizing or destabilizing climates. The interplay between stellar mass loss and planetary orbital decay creates feedback loops that either sustain or erode habitability.

**2.4 Case Study: Proxima Centauri’s Planetary System**
Proxima b orbits within its star’s habitable zone but endures frequent flares. Stellar wind erosion over 5+ Gyr challenges atmospheric retention, illustrating how magnetic activity and stellar evolution jointly dictate planetary fate.

**2.5 Predictive Modeling of Long-Term Evolution**
Numerical simulations project stellar mass loss rates and planetary orbital decay with increasing precision. Incorporating entropy gradients refines predictions of atmospheric loss, climate stability, and potential biosignatures.

**2.6 Implications for Exoplanet Surveys**
Targeted observations prioritize systems with stable, long-lived stars. Understanding temporal influences guides selection criteria for biosignature detection and informs models of planetary system evolution across cosmic time.

**2.7 Synthesis and Open Questions**
Temporal dynamics bridge stellar physics and planetary science. Key unresolved issues include quantifying magnetic field impacts on early Mars and modeling atmospheric stripping during red giant phases. Future missions aim to resolve these gaps through multi-messenger observations and high-resolution simulations.
