# Quantum Superposition in Curved Spacetime

In regions of strong gravitational curvature, quantum superposition behaves differently than in flat spacetime. The presence of a massive celestial body (e.g., Kether-9) warps spacetime, altering the probability amplitudes of quantum states. This curvature introduces **fracture points**—locations where superposed histories decohere into branching timelines.

## Fracture Points and Branching Probability

The likelihood of a timeline splitting at these points is governed by:

\[
P_{\text{branch}} = \frac{1}{Z} e^{-\beta E_b}
\]

Where:
- \(E_b\) is the energy barrier separating histories
- \(\beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}\) (inverse thermal energy)
- \(Z\) is the partition function normalizing probabilities

**Example:** During Kether-9’s accretion disc collapse, photons from future states arrived 12 hours early due to spacetime distortion. The branching probability determined whether these causal echoes persisted or collapsed.

## Non-Commuting Histories

Branched timelines interact through **non-commutative operators**, allowing past and future states to influence each other. This enables:
- Time-resolved events (e.g., supernova triggers)
- Quantum entanglement across fracture points
- Causal loops where later states retroactively alter earlier conditions

The interaction strength decays as \(e^{-\alpha \Delta t}\), where \(\Delta t\) is the temporal separation and \(\alpha\) depends on curvature radius.
