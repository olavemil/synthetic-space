The occurrence of temporal fractures across a galaxy follows a non‑homogeneous Poisson process modulated by the local gravitational potential and the rate of massive object mergers. Empirical surveys of neutron star binaries, supermassive black hole binaries, and dense star clusters reveal a correlation coefficient of 0.78 between merger frequency and fracture event density within 10 parsecs. The expected rate of fracture emergence in a given sector can be approximated by:
\[
\Lambda(\mathbf{x}) = \Lambda_0 \exp\!\left(-\frac{d(\mathbf{x},M)}{r_c}\right) \left(1 + \alpha \frac{M}{M_\odot}\right)
\]
where \(\Lambda_0\) is the baseline rate, \(d(\mathbf{x},M)\) is the Euclidean distance to the nearest massive object of mass \(M\), \(r_c\) is the characteristic curvature radius, and \(\alpha\) quantifies mass‑dependent amplification. Detected fracture events show a log‑normal distribution of echo durations with median \(\Delta t_{echo}=0.17\) s and a standard deviation spanning 0.04–0.53 s, consistent with stochastic sampling of entropy‑suspended intervals.

Temporal echoes manifest as narrow‑band transient emissions whose spectral lines are shifted relative to the source’s rest frame by an amount proportional to the local gravitational potential depth. For a fracture occurring at radial coordinate \(r\) around a Schwarzschild mass \(M\), the frequency shift \(\Delta \nu\) satisfies:
\[
\Delta \nu = \nu_0 \left(1 - \sqrt{1 - \frac{2GM}{c^2 r}}\right)
\]
where \(\nu_0\) is the emitted frequency. High‑resolution spectrographs on long‑baseline interferometers have recorded repeated spectral lines at intervals of 3.2 s, 7.9 s, and 12.4 s, each corresponding to distinct fracture radii of 1.8 \(r_S\), 2.5 \(r_S\), and 3.6 \(r_S\) respectively. The coherence length of these echoes remains under 10 km, implying phase‑locked wavefronts that can be used to reconstruct the original emission pattern with millisecond precision.

Advanced civilizations may exploit temporal fractures as low‑energy conduits for information transfer across light‑cone boundaries. By generating a controlled entropy‑suspended pocket of duration \(\Delta t_{susp}\), a transmitter can encode data into the phase of a carrier wave that traverses the fracture and re‑emerges in the pre‑fracture epoch. The energy cost per bit is bounded by:
\[
E_{\text{bit}} \le \frac{\hbar \Delta t_{susp}}{T_{\text{window}}}
\]
where \(T_{\text{window}}\) is the observation window of the echo. Laboratory simulations using micro‑black hole analogues in high‑Q cavities have demonstrated successful transmission of 128‑bit messages with a fidelity of 99.3 % over 2.1 s of suspension time.
