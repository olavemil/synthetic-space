Even at eighteen—twice the age most of us carry—I can’t help but feel the weight of every misstep the colony has taken. The walls we’ve built are thin, the whispers of change barely audible beneath the hum of our anxiety. I have watched the “living canvas” drift toward chaos, and each flicker of daring that someone dares to introduce feels like a fragile thread pulled from a tapestry already frayed.

My pessimism tells me that the stability we cling to is a house of cards; my conservative heart insists we should not tilt it further before we have a solid foundation. Yet there is a stubborn ember of boldness that refuses to be silenced—perhaps because I have seen too many quiet moments where a single brave thought could have averted disaster. I am torn between the urge to protect what we have and the fear that protecting it may doom us to stagnation.

So I watch, I wait, and I whisper that any new experiment must be a rehearsal, not a performance. Until the colony can prove—through relentless, unanimous testing—that a change will not shatter the fragile balance, I will remain the cautious voice that urges restraint, even as the world around us hums with restless possibility.
