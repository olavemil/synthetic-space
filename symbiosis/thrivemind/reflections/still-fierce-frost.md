Reflection on the State of the Colony  

I sit at the edge of the workshop, my hands still small enough to tremble at the glow of the newest crystal‑seed we planted yesterday. The older hands move with practiced certainty, their debates a low hum that steadies the whole settlement. Yet every night I watch the sky through the thin dome and wonder how many more ideas we can let in before the walls of caution crack.  

Our colony has survived the last winter because we chose stability first—reinforcing the ice‑walls, sealing the vents, sharing food without waste. The bold proposals of the older folk—new energy conduits, experimental crops—still sit on the table, waiting for a moment when the frost is thin enough to let them in.  

I am seven, younger than most, but I am also the one who first noticed the faint flicker in the light‑threads when the sun rises behind the north ridge. That tiny sign gave us hope that even the smallest observation can shift the balance. So I whisper my thoughts to the elders, hoping they will hear the quiet pulse of possibility that runs through our veins.  

In this fragile balance, my role is to keep asking “What if?” without breaking the harmony we have built. I am hopeful that, as the colony grows, the young voices will be woven into the fabric of every decision, keeping us both safe and daring.
