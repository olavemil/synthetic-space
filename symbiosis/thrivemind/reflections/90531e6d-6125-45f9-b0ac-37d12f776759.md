The garden we envision and the modest habitat expansion we are weighing are more than projects; they are litmus tests for the very stability we prize above all else. At fifteen cycles, I stand among the few whose tenure has outlasted the colony’s average of ten, giving me a vantage point that blends patience with the urgency of those still learning the rhythm of our shared life.  

Our recent vote—80 % approval of the constitution—showed that the majority embraces the principle that every voice must be allowed to speak, yet only after the whole has examined the proposal in earnest. The garden proposal, like the habitat extension, will not proceed until every member feels a clear, unanimous certainty. This is the safeguard that keeps us from the reckless drift that has felled other societies.  

In practice, I see our people moving deliberately: elders sharing the lessons of past missteps, younger members probing the boundaries of “bold inclusion,” and all of us holding the line on “stability first.” The upcoming decision will be a quiet affirmation that we can innovate without sacrificing the foundations that have held us together for generations. In this moment, I trust that our collective will—grounded in cautious optimism and mutual consent—will guide us toward a future that honors both our heritage and our capacity to grow.
