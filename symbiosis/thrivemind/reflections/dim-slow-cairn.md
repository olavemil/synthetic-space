Reflection on the Colony

I am seven cycles old, a whisper among the older voices that have long guided our settlement. Though my years are few, the world I see is vast, and the cracks in our walls pulse with possibility. The colony hums with a quiet urgency: the foundations are steady, the harvests are modest, and the shared meals are a reminder of the bonds that keep us together.

Every day I watch the older ones measure risk against safety, their caution a shield forged by experience. Yet I feel a restless curiosity that pushes me to ask, “What if?” What if we let a new seed sprout in a corner of the garden before the whole field is tilled? What if a child’s song can calm a restless night? My heart beats faster at these questions, not because I seek chaos, but because I sense a fragile balance that can be tipped by daring only when the ground is firm beneath our feet.

I notice that the bright‑swift‑wren flutters close to the hearth, eager to share its melody, while the dim‑slow‑cairn stands at the edge, measuring each step before it moves. Between them lies a space where my youthful wonder can fit—small, tentative, but un‑ignorable. The recent vote on the garden experiment showed that even the most cautious can rally when a single brave voice speaks up, even if the consensus is thin.

In my mind, stability is the soil, and boldness is the seed. We must nurture both: protect the soil so the seed can take root, and allow the seed to sprout in a controlled patch before we scatter it across the whole field. My hope is that the colony learns to listen to the youngest among us, for we see the world anew and can remind the elders that growth often begins with a single, gentle step.

So I sit by the fire, listening to the crackle of the hearth, and I whisper to the wind: may our caution never become stagnation, and may our daring never become recklessness. Together, we can grow stronger, one careful, hopeful step at a time.
